## 1. Why Bearings Fail — Statistical Overview

Of all bearings in service, only 0.5% fail prematurely. Of those that do fail:
- **1/3 fail due to fatigue** (overload, contamination-induced, or incorrect fit)
- **1/3 fail due to lubrication problems** (wrong type, insufficient quantity, degraded)
- **1/6 fail due to contamination** (particles, moisture, chemicals)
- **1/6 fail for other reasons** (improper handling, incorrect mounting, misalignment)

**Diagnostic implication:** When a bearing fault frequency is identified, the most probable root causes in order of likelihood are lubrication failure, contamination, and overload — not random fatigue. The vibration trend and bearing damage pattern together identify which.

## 2. Maintenance Methodology Framework

| Methodology | Description | Bearing Condition as Input? |
|---|---|---|
| Reactive | Fix when broken; no advance warning | No |
| Preventive | Replace at fixed intervals regardless of condition | No |
| Predictive (PdM) | Monitor condition; schedule based on findings | Yes |
| Proactive Reliability Maintenance | Combines PdM with root cause correction | Yes + root cause |
| Operator Driven Reliability (ODR) | Front-line operators own and communicate machine health | Yes |

**SKF recommendation:** Proactive reliability maintenance — detect faults early using vibration analysis, then find and correct the root cause rather than just replacing the bearing.

**Key principle:** Replacing a damaged bearing after detecting high vibration only temporarily solves the problem. The root cause must be identified and corrected, or the next bearing will fail in the same way.

## 3. Vibration Monitoring — Principles and Practice

### Why Vibration Monitoring Works
- All machines vibrate
- The onset of a mechanical problem is almost always accompanied by an increase in vibration
- The nature of the fault can be determined from the vibration frequency characteristics

### Frequency Ranges and What They Indicate
| Frequency Range | Typical Sources |
|---|---|
| 0–2 kHz (low) | Structural resonance, imbalance, misalignment, looseness, gear mesh |
| 2–50 kHz (high) | Rolling bearing defects (early to mid-stage) |
| > 50 kHz (very high) | Earliest stage bearing defects, ultrasonic emission |

### Where to Take Measurements
Measurements should be taken in **three directions at each bearing position:**
- **Horizontal:** Most sensitive to imbalance (machine is typically more flexible horizontally). Excessive horizontal vibration = often imbalance.
- **Vertical:** Complementary to horizontal for radial fault identification
- **Axial:** Typically low; if elevated, indicates misalignment or bent shaft

### When to Take Measurements
- When machine is at normal operating temperature and speed
- For variable-speed machines: always at the same point in the process cycle
- Record same location, same direction, same operating conditions every measurement — this is essential for trend analysis

### Detection Sensitivity — Comparison
| Method | Detection Capability | Stage Detected |
|---|---|---|
| Listen and feel | Detects only severe damage | Stage 4 (near failure) |
| Standard vibration monitoring (velocity) | Moderate damage | Stage 3 |
| Enveloped acceleration (SKF gE) | Very early damage | Stage 1–2 |

**Conclusion:** Standard velocity-based monitoring misses early bearing faults. Envelope/demodulation analysis is required to detect Stages 1–2. This is why AxiomAnare uses demod (g RMS) alongside velocity (mm/s RMS) as separate channels.

## 4. Rolling Bearing Defect Frequency Analysis

### How Bearing Defect Frequencies Work
Every bearing component has a unique defect frequency based on:
- Number and size of rolling elements
- Bearing contact angle
- Rolling element pitch diameter
- Shaft rotational speed

When a defect is over-rolled by a rolling element, a high-frequency impulse is generated. The rate of these impulses is the bearing defect frequency.

### Defect Frequency Types
| Symbol | Description |
|---|---|
| BPFO | Ball/Roller Pass Frequency Outer ring (raceway) |
| BPFI | Ball/Roller Pass Frequency Inner ring (raceway) |
| BSF | Ball/Roller Spin Frequency |
| FTF | Cage Frequency (Fundamental Train Frequency) |

### Enveloped Acceleration Analysis
Enveloping isolates the high-frequency signal generated by a defect from other naturally occurring rotational and structural frequencies. This makes bearing fault frequencies visible at an early stage — long before they appear in the standard velocity spectrum.

Practical output: Spectrum peaks at BPFO, 2×BPFO, 3×BPFO (or BPFI, BSF equivalents) in the enveloped spectrum confirm bearing fault location.

## 5. Condition Monitoring During Operation

### Key Parameters to Monitor
| Parameter | Instrument Type | Indicates |
|---|---|---|
| Noise | Electronic stethoscope, ultrasonic probe | Early bearing damage, lubrication failure, looseness |
| Temperature | Contact or non-contact thermometer, thermal imager | Lubrication failure, overload, excessive friction, seal drag |
| Vibration | Accelerometer, vibration analyser | All mechanical fault types |
| Lubrication condition | Grease test kit, oil analysis | Contamination, degradation, viscosity breakdown |

### Temperature Monitoring Notes
- Normal: bearing housing is typically 5°C cooler than outer ring, 10°C cooler than inner ring
- Temperature rise after relubrication is normal — lasts 1–2 days then returns to baseline
- If temperature does not return to normal after relubrication → bearing is over-greased or has another fault
- Unexpected temperature increase without change in operating conditions = early warning of bearing damage

### Lubrication Inspection Checklist
1. Check for lubricant leaks at all bearing positions
2. Examine leaks — worn seals, seal defects, liquid contamination, loose plugs, housing joint gaps
3. Keep labyrinth seals and protective collars filled with grease
4. Verify automatic lubrication systems are functioning and delivering correct quantity
5. Check lubricant level in sumps and reservoirs
6. Regrease at appropriate intervals per relubrication schedule

## 6. Inspection During Shutdown

### Bearing Visual Inspection Procedure
1. Clean external machine surfaces to prevent contamination ingress
2. Remove housing cover to expose bearing
3. Take lubricant sample for analysis before cleaning
4. Inspect exposed bearing surfaces for fretting corrosion — remove with fine abrasive paper if not severe
5. Inspect bearing rings for cracks
6. Rotate shaft slowly by hand — undamaged bearing rotates smoothly with even resistance
7. If detailed inspection required: clean with petroleum-based solvent, dry with lint-free cloth
8. Use endoscope to inspect raceways, cage, and rolling elements for spalls, scratches, discolouration, mirror-like areas
9. Measure radial internal clearance — confirm within specification
10. If satisfactory: regrease and close up. If damaged: dismount and conduct full damage analysis.

### What to Look For on a Dismounted Bearing
| Observation | Likely Cause | Vibration Signature Correlation |
|---|---|---|
| Localised spalling on outer race (one zone) | Point load, fatigue | BPFO harmonics, progression through stages |
| Spalling distributed around outer race | Rotating outer ring (ring creep) | BPFO with amplitude modulation |
| Spalling on inner race with sidebands in spectrum | Rotating inner ring under radial load | BPFI with 1X sidebands |
| Regular ridges or channels on raceway | Electric current / fluting | BPFO at very high frequency |
| Pitting / corrosion on rolling surfaces | Moisture ingress, acid contamination | Irregular BPFI/BPFO pattern |
| Denting / indentations at ball spacing | False brinelling from static vibration | Sub-harmonic patterns |
| Particle denting (rough surface, fine indentations) | Contaminated lubricant | Broadband noise + irregular fault frequencies |
| Smearing (mirror-like streaks) | Roller skidding, lubricant film breakdown | Elevated broadband noise, heat |
| Fretting debris (reddish-brown) at fits | Inner/outer ring creep | 1X sidebands on all bearing frequencies |

## 7. Bearing Failure Root Cause Matrix

| Root Cause | Bearing Damage Mode | Vibration Indicator | Corrective Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Insufficient lubrication | Wear, smearing, early spalling | Rapid fault progression, elevated temperatures | Review relubrication interval and quantity |
| Wrong lubricant | Metal-to-metal contact, thermal damage | High noise, temperature spikes | Verify viscosity grade and NLGI rating for application |
| Lubricant contamination (particles) | Particle denting → fatigue spalling | Broadband noise + accelerating BPFO/BPFI trend | Improve sealing; flush and regrease |
| Lubricant contamination (water) | Corrosion, pitting | Irregular BPFO/BPFI; pitting signature | Fix water ingress source; change to water-resistant grease |
| Misalignment | Uneven load distribution; accelerated fatigue | BPFI elevated on one side; 1X, 2X axial | Correct alignment; re-measure |
| Imbalance | Increased dynamic load on bearings | 1X elevated radially; all bearing fault frequencies elevated | Balance rotor |
| Overload | Subsurface fatigue; spalling | Shortened time to Stage 3–4 from normal | Review load conditions; check for added belt drives or coupling changes |
| Incorrect fit (too loose) | Ring creep; fretting | 1X sidebands on bearing frequencies; fretting debris | Remachine shaft/bore to correct tolerance |
| Incorrect fit (too tight) | Reduced clearance; overheating; fatigue | Elevated temperatures; accelerated spalling | Replace bearing with correct clearance for fit |
| Electric current | Fluting | BPFO at very high frequency (100k–180k CPM) | Install shaft grounding; check VFD earthing |
| Mounting damage | Impact denting; misassembly | Early fault frequencies; noise from installation | Use correct mounting tools and procedure |

## 8. Oil Analysis — Key Parameters and Thresholds

| Parameter | Normal | Action Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Viscosity | Within 10% of baseline value | > 10% deviation → investigate |
| Water content | < 200 ppm | > 200 ppm → investigate water ingress |
| Particle count (ISO 4406) | ≤ 18/15/12 (automatic counting) | Above this → improve filtration |
| Oxidation | Low | High oxidation → change oil; review temperature |

**Crackle test for water:** Heat surface to 130°C, place drop of oil — visible vapour bubbles indicate water presence. Crackling sound = likely > 2,000 ppm water.

## 9. Alignment — Impact on Bearing Condition

Alignment of drives and driven machinery is essential for:
- Preventing premature bearing damage
- Reducing vibration and stress levels
- Reducing friction losses and noise
- Extending seal and coupling life

**Alignment targets should be set before taking baseline vibration readings.** Misalignment generates bearing load patterns that produce vibration signatures identical to bearing damage — correct alignment first, then assess bearing condition.

### Misalignment and Bearing Fault Interaction
- Angular misalignment: generates axial thrust on bearings → BPFI on thrust-loaded bearing
- Parallel misalignment: generates alternating radial load → 2X dominant, elevated bearing load
- Combined misalignment: multiple load zones → both BPFI and BPFO may be elevated
- A bearing showing Stage 2–3 fault frequencies on a recently replaced bearing = alignment has not been corrected

## 10. Diagnostic Decision Tree for Bearing Faults

```
Bearing fault frequency detected?
    │
    ├── Stage 1–2 (demod only, no velocity peak)
    │       → Schedule inspection at next planned maintenance
    │       → Check lubrication condition and relubrication interval
    │       → Trend monthly
    │
    ├── Stage 3 (velocity spectrum, BPFO/BPFI visible)
    │       → Investigate root cause NOW before scheduling replacement
    │       → Check: alignment, balance, lubrication, contamination, fit
    │       → Replace bearing and correct root cause at same maintenance event
    │
    └── Stage 4 (broadband noise, fault frequencies absent or random)
            → Urgent replacement — risk of catastrophic failure
            → Do not operate unmonitored
            → Post-replacement: inspect housing, shaft, and root cause
```
